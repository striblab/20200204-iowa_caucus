How Amy Klobuchar performed at the Iowa Caucus

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune February 10, 2020

Despite a chaotic Iowa Caucus night leading to days of delayed results and widespread controversy about their veracity, Minnesota's Sen. Amy Klobuchar still fell short of her hoped performance.

Klobuchar has touted dominant electoral performances in Minnesota throughout places President Donald Trump won in 2016. But that kind of support didn't translate into an Iowa victory, opening questions to how she'll do in Tuesday's New Hampshire Primary, where voter demographics are similar.

Her campaign to convince Iowa's Democratic balloters of her presidential viability ended with a fifth-place finish, and she was allocated a single pledged national delegate for the Democratic National Convention in Milwaukee this July.

Despite visiting all 99 counties in Iowa, Klobuchar's support was strongest in rural and some suburban areas, while beeing mostly shut out of larger cities like Des Moines and Cedar Rapids, which is more plainly revealed through precinct-level maps of the caucus results.

These maps only show Klobuchar's allocation of State Delegate Equivalents. When measured against all her rivals, she primarily led tallies in sparsely-populated areas of the state, while being washed out nearly everywhere else.

Though New Hampshire polls show a slight surge for Klobuchar following Friday's DNC debate, she still lags behind party frontrunners like South Bend, Ind. Mayor Pete Buttigieg and Vermont Sen. Bernie Sanders, who each claimed victories amid Iowa's murky outcome. Some polls, however, put Klobuchar ahead of both Massachusetts Sen. Elizabeth Warren and former Vice President Joe Biden, both of whom she trailed in Iowa.

In Iowa, caucusgoers voted for their preferred candidate at each precinct, and those candidates reaching at least 15 percent moved onto another round. The results of the second round ended with candidates being granted State Delegate Equivalents based on a mathematical formula, which are then translated into pledged national delegates.

New Hampshire, meanwhile, conducts a more straightforward primary, where 24 national delegates are awarded among candidates proportionally based on their performance.

Following the controversies surrounding Iowa's outcome, New Hampshire's vote is something of a reset for the Democratic campaigns and may offer Klobuchar a fresh chance at a stronger showing in the type of purple state where she's argued her electoral advantage lies.
